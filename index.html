PwC Call Center Performance - BI Case Study

Using data to relieve a call center queue bottleneck

Over three months the center handled 5,000 calls. Once callers reach an agent, performance is strong: 81% of calls are resolved and satisfaction averages 3.4/5. The constraint is the queue. The dashboard below turns that pattern into concrete actions on scheduling, call-backs and self-service.

Dashboard

Call Center Performance Dashboard

The report is built as three focused pages: a main dashboard, topical analysis, and operations by time. Together they connect executive KPIs to queues, topics, and staffing patterns.

Over the period, demand is steady at 675-770 calls per day. Staffing is flat at eight agents per day, and demand clusters between 11:00 and 15:00. In this window SLA is weakest and abandonment is highest. SLA by topic sits between 7-10%, and agent metrics occupy a narrow band for SLA, answer speed and satisfaction. In short: the constraint is how work and capacity are organised, not a specific queue or group of agents.

The report turns that diagnosis into an operating plan:

  • Rebuild the schedule around the 11:00-15:00 peak to bring peak-time answer speed down toward 40-45 seconds.
  • Introduce a call-back offer beyond a wait-time threshold (e.g., 60 seconds) to convert a portion of the 946 abandoned calls into planned conversations.
  • Streamline high-volume topics such as Streaming, Technical Support and Payment with short IVR pre-screening and self-service for common requests.
  • Refine handle-time measurement (talk time plus wrap-up by interval) so staffing models target the specific hours where capacity changes will move SLA.
Focus on SLA & abandonment in the queue Uses agent data for coaching, not blame Highlights 11:00-15:00 as the critical interval

The solution is built in Power BI on top of a call-level fact table. The model separates demand, capacity and service outcomes so each can be analysed independently.

  • Model: star schema with FactCalls and dimensions for Agent, Topic, Date and Time.
  • Measures: DAX measures for Total Calls, Answered Calls, Answer Rate, Avg Answer Speed, Abandoned Calls, Abandonment Rate and SLA Within Target.
  • SLA logic: configurable SLA threshold (20 seconds in this report) with a measure for “calls answered within SLA” and the corresponding percentage.
  • Visuals: KPI cards, volume/SLA trend line, SLA % by topic, calls by topic, operations-by-time heatmaps and performance-by-agent tables.

Data can be refreshed from SQL or CSV exports. Power Query handles cleaning, time-bucket creation and topic mapping before the data reaches the model.

Report pages

Each tab is designed for a slightly different audience: leaders use the main dashboard; operational managers spend more time in the topical and time-based views.

Main Dashboard

High-level view of demand, SLA, abandonment and satisfaction, with a trend line that compares call volume and SLA over time.

  • Explains the gap between strong resolution (81%) and weak SLA (7.5% for all calls, 9.2% for answered calls).
  • Gives leaders a single place to track abandonment and satisfaction together.
  • Acts as the hub page that links into deeper topical and operational analysis.

Topical Analysis

Focuses on how Streaming, Technical Support, Payment, Admin and Contract calls differ in volume, SLA and satisfaction.

  • Identifies which topics create the most pressure on the queue.
  • Highlights where pre-screening and self-service will have the highest impact.
  • Supports conversation design work with product and CX teams.

Operations by Time

Heatmaps and staffing diagnostics show when the centre is overloaded and where next changes to shifts will move SLA and abandonment.

  • Points to 11:00-15:00 as the period where demand outstrips flat staffing.
  • Uses total talk time and target occupancy to estimate required agents per time block.
  • Helps reallocate existing FTE before considering new headcount.

Business context

The dashboard groups metrics into demand, queue performance and agent outcomes. Looking at them together highlights that the main opportunity is in the queue and schedule rather than agent behaviour.

Example questions it answers

  • Where is demand outstripping capacity by hour or topic?
  • How much abandonment can we realistically remove with better scheduling?
  • Do agents perform consistently once customers get through to them?
Approach & skills

From raw call logs to an operating plan

The same approach applies across BI and analytics work: start from the decision, build a clean model, then use the visuals to test and refine the plan with stakeholders.

  • 1. Frame the decisions
    Align with team leaders on what must change: reduce abandonment, restore SLA, or rebalance workload. Translate those goals into measurable targets and a clear scope for the dashboard.
  • 2. Build a robust data model
    Restructure call logs into a star schema. Define consistent measures for volume, service levels, wait times and satisfaction so every visual tells the same story.
  • 3. Expose the operational levers
    Design visuals that separate demand from capacity: call volume and SLA trends, weekday and time-of-day patterns, topic mix and agent metrics. This makes the impact of schedule changes or call-back rules visible.
  • 4. Turn insight into actions
    Use the dashboard in working sessions to test options such as shifting agents into the 11:00-15:00 peak, setting a call-back threshold, or simplifying high-volume flows. Document the agreed actions alongside the metrics they aim to move.
